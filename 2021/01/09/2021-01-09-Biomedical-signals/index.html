Biomedical signals

Video

  • Or bio-signal
  • In theory
    • Any measurement over time of a living being
  • In practice
    • Closely related to physiological processes, allowing monitoring or detection
  • Wide range of measurement principals
    • Electric, magnetic, impedance, optical, mechanical , …
  • Here, focus on EEG, EMG and EKG/ECG – Electrical measurements

Bioelectricity

Definition: The generation or action of electric currents or voltages in biological processes

Bioelectric phenomena: Fast signalling in nerves and the triggering of physical processes in muscles or glands

Electric activity in living tissue

  • A cellular phenomenon, dependent on the cell membrane
  • The membrane acts like a capacitor, storing energy as electrically charged ions on opposite sides of the membrane

Cell polarisation

The membrane potential

Membrane potential refers to the difference in electrical charge between the inside and outside of a neuron. This is the 质膜plasma or 细胞膜cell membrane of the neuron.

它将细胞内部与外部环境隔开；我们会说这是神经元的内部，这是神经元的外部。电荷差异是由于膜内外离子的grouping而产生的。

离子是失去或获得电子并因此带有正电荷或负电荷的原子。

人体内发现有多种离子；有几种在神经元的膜电位中起重要作用。当神经元处于静止状态时，钠离子和氯离子在细胞外更为普遍。还有带正电的钾离子，用这些黄色圆圈表示，还有各种带负电的离子，通常称为有机阴离子（阴离子只是带负电的离子的简称）。

当神经元处于静止状态时，钾离子和有机阴离子在细胞内更为普遍。静止时，神经元内部比外部带更多负电荷，导致平均神经元的静息膜电位约为 -70 mV。

保持这种潜力的一种方式是通过一种称为钠钾泵sodium-potassium pump的机制。这是一种运输蛋白，它利用能量不断地将三个钠离子泵出细胞，同时将两个钾离子泵入细胞。由于泵出的正离子多于负离子，因此有助于保持膜电位为负。

与其他离子不同，钾倾向于通过离子通道相当容易地穿过细胞膜，离子通道ion channels是允许离子通过的跨膜蛋白membrane spanning proteins。 钾会从神经元排出，直到达到平衡点——此时扩散等力不会将其推向一个方向或另一个方向。 此时，神经元的膜电位在-65～-70mV左右，称为静息膜resting membrane电位potential。

Cell membrane potentials (or resting potential)

Cells are polarized in resting state

  • Intracellular space has negative potential with respect to environment
    • Typically -70mV for a neuron cell

Controlled by the sodium-potassium pumps in the cell membrane

  • Caused by unequal distribution of Na+/K+

Active transport of Na+/K+ -ions across membrane

  • Against concentration gradient

  • 3 Na+ ejected in exchanged for 2 Ka+

    Results in a net negative cell charge

Sodium-Potassium pump

  • Hydrolysis of ATP to ADP
  • 3 Na+ outside the cell
  • 2 Ka+ inside the cell

Action potentials

Occurs in excitable cells

  • Neurons, muscle and endocrine cells

Ion channels in the membrane allow transport of ions

  • Electrically or chemically triggered to open

Cell depolarization is the process of shifting from negative to positive charge

  • E.g. Na-channels open, Na+ rushes back into the cell

During repolarization initial potential is established again

  • K-channels open, K+ flows out of the cell

Neurons and signalling

  • Cells found in central & peripheral nervous system
  • Wide variety in shape, size, and electrochemical properties
  • Principal parts
    • Dendrites Branch-like extensions-main input to the neuron
    • Soma Body of the neuron
    • Axon Cable-like extension – transporting the action potential away from the soma to other cells
    • Synapse Release of neurotransmitter chemicals
  • Neurons signal other cells using electro-chemical interactions

Action potential in neurons

Cells get excited

  • External stimulus (sensory neurons)
  • Other cells

Causes polarity change of membrane

  • Starts at axon
  • Triggers neighbouring membrane
  • Repolarization afterwards

Action potentials travel down the axon

  • One direction, to the axon terminal
  • Here it may signal other neurons
  • Corresponds to a (micro) current

Neurotransmission

Or synaptic transmission

  • Presynaptic neuron sends signals
    • Action potential travels down the axon
  • Synaptic vessels release neurotransmitter chemical
    • Drift across synaptic cleft
  • Ion gates of postsynaptic neuron open
    • Dendrites /soma receive postsynaptic potential
  • Depending on the neurotransmitter, postsynaptic potentials can be +/ -
    • Excitatory (EPSP): depolarising
    • Inhibitory (IPSP): hyperpolarizing
  • Neurotransmission can also occur electrically
    • Not discussed here

Effect of post-synaptic potentials

Neurons may receive several postsynaptic potentials

Effect is summed spatially and temporally

  • Repeated firing of one presynaptic neuron
  • Simultaneous firing of many presynaptic neurons

(A) Potential remains is below threshold

  • No reaction from postsynaptic neuron

(B) Potential above threshold

  • Depolarisation, action potential is fired

Special case of synapse

Neuromuscular junction

  • The axon of a motor neuron terminates on a muscle fiber
  • The released neurotransmitter binds to an integral protein in the membrane, the sarcolemma
  • The sarcolemma surrounds the muscle fiber

Allows a motor neuron to transmit a signal to the muscle fiber

  • Causing muscle contraction

The human brain

Principal organ of the central nerve system

  • 1010 neurons in the brain

Main tissue types

  • Ventricles
    • Production of cerebrospinal fluid
  • White matter
    • Connections to different parts of grey matter
    • Corpus callosum

    • High density of neurons
    • Outer layer also called **cortex

Sources of EEG

  • Cortex

    • Folded structure
      • Greater area, more connections
    • Different cortical layers
      • Contain characteristic distribution of neuron types

  • Pyramidal cells

    • Long neurons with conic soma

    • Regularly oriented perpendicular to cortical surface

    • Generate coherent electric & magnetic field

      • When triggered with degree of synchrony

    • Main source of EEG signals

      • ~105 simultaneously active neurons needed for measurable EEG signal

What are we measuring?

Postsynaptic potentials are primary source of the EEG signals

  • Action Potentials
    • Large variation (± 100mV)
    • Short duration (± 1ms)
    • Generally not synchronized
  • Postsynaptic potentials
    • Small variation (± 10mV)
    • Long duration (± 10ms)
    • Synchronized, effects can add up

EEG electrodes

  • Made of conductive material
    • Galvanic connection between electrode and biological tissue
  • Electrode-tissue junction must not introduce noise or distortions
    • Low impedance to limit distortion
    • High quality conductive materials
      • High-purity gold (Au)
      • Chlorided silver (AgCl)
      • Sintered Ag/AgCl
  • Active electrodes for lower noise levels
    • Contain low-noise amplifier near skin

Electrode placement

  • Electrode placement on the scalp
    • Sometimes on cortical surface
  • Using standardized placement system
    • International 10-20 system based on 21 electrodes
    • High-density readings using 32-64-128 measurements
  • Using a registration cap
    • Improve reproducibility of the placement

EEG Instrumentation

Example of EEG block diagram

  • Projection circuit
    • Protect circuit from input; and user from failing circuitry
  • Instrumentation amplifier
    • High quality amplification
  • Staged amplification
    • Remove DC offset using high pass filter
  • Low-pass filter
    • Prevent aliasing when the signal is digitized
  • Right-leg driver (electrode)
    • Reduces common mode noise
    • 50Hz-60Hz hum

Artifacts and noise

EEG is a faint signal, prone to noise and artefacts

  • Environment noise
    • 50Hz-60Hz power line activity
  • Instrumentation noise
    • Quantization noise
  • Biological noise
    • Eye movement and blinking, muscle activity, ECG signal

Used for diagnosis and monitoring

  • Epilepsy, sleep disorders, coma, …

Epilepsy

  • Epilepsy is the fourth most common neurological disorder and affects people of all ages
  • Means the same thing as "seizure disorders"
  • Characterized by unpredictable seizures and can cause other health problems.
  • A spectrum condition with a wide range of seizure types and control varying from person-to-person

The electrocardiogram (ECG)

not for exam

The P wave is the first deflection of a normal ECG waveform. It represents the atrial depolarization started at the SA node as well as the conduction of the electrical impulse through the atria. Normal P waves precede QRS complexes and last 60 ms to 120 ms. In most leads, normal P waves are rounded and upright with amplitudes from 0.2 mV to 0.3 mV.

The PR/PQ interval corresponds to the travel of the electrical impulse from its generation in the SA node through the internodal tract, AV node, the bundle of His and left and right bundle branches. Normal PR interval is located from t he beginning of the P-wave to the beginning of the Q wave and lasts typically 120 ms to 200 ms.

The QRS complex represents the depolarization of the ventricles. It is composed of three waves, the Q wave, the R wave and the S wave. Not all of these waves have to be present in a normal QRS. The QRS complexes follow the PR intervals and their amplitudes may vary depending on the lead used. The QRS is measured from the beginning of the Q wave to the end of the S wave (the J point) and lasts in the range of 60 ms to 100 ms.

The ST segment represents the interval between the end of the ventricular depolarization and the beginning of the ventricular repolarization. Normal ST segments start at the J point and are usually isoelectric.

The T wave corresponds to the ventricular repolarization. Normal T wave follows the S wave and its amplitude varies depending on the lead. A non- pathological T wave is typically round and smooth as shown in the figure ->

The U wave when it is present follows the T wave. Although the U wave genesis has been associated to several hypotheses its origin remains unclear. It has been observed in hypokalemia and hypercalcemia but also in young athletes.

On the one hand, the QT interval is the time elapsed from the beginning of the ventricular depolarization (Q onset) to the end of the ventricular repolarization (T offset). On the other hand, the RR interval is the time between two consecutive R peaks. The analysis of both intervals has found several applications in medical practice.

Digital signal filtering

System response as a convolution

A discrete LTI system \[ y[n] = Lx[n] \] with \[ h[n] = L\delta [n]\\ L\delta [n -k] = h[n -k] \] An arbitrary input \[ x[n] = \sum^{+∞}_{k=-∞}x[k]\delta[n-k]\\ L\sum^{+∞}_{k=-∞}x[k]\delta[n-k]=\sum^{+∞}_{k=-∞}x[k]h[n-k] \] The system response can be found as the convolution of the input with the impulse response \[ y[n] =\sum^{+∞}_{i=-∞}x[i]h[n-i]\\ y[n] =\sum^{+∞}_{i=-∞}x[n-i]h[i]\\ y[n] = x[n] *h[n] \]

Fourier series of LTI systems

  • Complex exponential on the input \[ x[n] = e^{j\omega t_n} \]

    • Functions of this type are eigenfunctions of the LTI system

  • The system response can be written as a convolution

  • The output is a complex exponential weighted by a complex gain factor H（⍵）

    • H（⍵） is an eigenvalue and is dependent on frequency of the input signal x[n]

  • For a linear combination of complex exponentials \[ x[n]=\sum_kc_ke^{j\omega_kt_n}\\ y[n]=\sum_kc_kH(\omega_k)e^{j\omega_kt_n} \]

    • If the input can be represented as a linear combination of complex exponentials
      • Using Fourier transform, any signal can be written in this form
    • Then the output can also be represented with a complex exponentials
      • Each linear coefficient is scaled according to the frequency
      • The output of the filter depends on the frequency

Filters

  • Every sinusoidal component is modified
    • Amplitude and phase multiplied with the corresponding value of H（f）
  • When we eliminate or supress frequencies, the LTI-system is called a frequency-band filter
    • Named by how it changes the frequency spectrum

Z-transform

Definition:

  • Converts a discrete-time signal, which is a sequence of real or complex numbers, into a complex frequency domain representation.
  • It can be considered as a discrete-time equivalent of the Laplace transform (~Fourier transform).

The Z-transform is defined as \[ X(z)=\sum^{∞}_{0}x(n)z^{-n} \]

  • where X(z) is the transformed signal
  • x(n) is the original signal

Let z = e-jω , i.e. a complex exponential \[ X(e^{-j\omega})=\sum^{∞}_{0}x(n)e^{-jn\omega} \]

  • The Z-transform becomes the Fourier transform
  • But offers a compact and convenient notation

Shift Theorem

A delay of Δ samples in the time domain corresponds to the multiplication of z-Δ in the Z-domain.

  • Z{shift∆ {x}} = z-ΔX(z) for ∆ ≥ 0
  • x(n-Δ) ↔︎ z-ΔX(z)

Proof

Example

So, if we have a filter given by \[ y(n)=x(n)+g_1x(n-M_1)-g_2y(n-M_2) \] Then \[ Z\{y(n)\}=X(z)+g_1Z^{-M_1}X(z)-g_2Z^{-M_2}Y(z) \]

Transfer function using the Z-transform

  • Impulse response & Frequency response

    • Now in the Z-domain

  • \[ H(z)=\frac{Y(z)}{X(z)} \]

    • Where X(z) is the z-transform of the input signal
    • Y(z) is the z-transform of the output signal.

Example

In general (infinite impulse resonse IIR filter)

\[ y(n)=b_0x(n)+b_1x(n-1)+...+b_Mx(n-M)-a_1y(n-1)-...-a_Ny(n-N)\\ Z\{y(.)\}=Z\{b_0x(n)+b_1x(n-1)+...+b_Mx(n-M)-a_1y(n-1)-...-a_Ny(n-N)\}\\ Y(z)=b_0X(z)+b_1z^{-1}X(z)+...+b_Mz^{-M}X(z)-a_1z^{-1}Y(z)-...-a_Nz^{-N}Y(z)\\ \]

\[ H(z)=\frac{Y(z)}{X(z)}=\frac{b_0+b_1z^{-1}+...+b_MNz^{-M}}{1+a_1z^{-1}+...+a_Nz^{-N}} \]

Filtering in frequency domain

Principle

  • Input signal in time domain
    • The original time-series
  • Convert signal to frequency domain
    • Fourier transform
  • Filter / select the frequencies you want to keep
    • Multiply with a rectangular pulse
  • Transform back to time domain
    • Inverse Fourier transform
  • The output is a low / band / high-pass filtered signal

Filtering in temporal domain

  • For example, using a moving average filter

    5-point moving average

    • \[ y[n] = \frac{1}{5}(x[0] + x[n − 1] + x[n − 2] + x[n − 3] + x[n − 4]) = 0.2(x[0] + x[n − 1] + x[n − 2] + x[n − 3] + x[n − 4]) \]

    • \[ y[n]=0.2(x[0]+x[n−1]+x[n−2]+x[n−3]+x[n−4]) \]

  • Z-transform

    • \[ Y[z]=0.2(z^{0}+z^{−1}+z^{−2}+z^{−3}+z^{−4})X[z] \]

    • \[ Y[z]=0.2(1+z^{−1}+z^{−2}+z^{−3}+z^{−4})X[z] \]

Summary

Bioelectricity

Cell membranes are polarized

  • Potential is maintained by sodium-potassium pumps

Excitable cells can be depolarized

  • Resulting in an action potential

Neurons can signal other cells

  • Action potentials triggering release of neurotransmitter chemicals at the synaptic cleft

Neuron signals can excitatory or inhibitory

  • Promoting a depolarisation of the post-synaptic neuron, or not

Muscle cells

  • Can also exhibit an action potential, causing the muscle to contract

Electroencephalography (EEG)

  • The grey matter has the highest density of neurons
  • Pyramidal cells in the cortex are the main source of EEG → 105 simultaneous post-synaptic currents
  • Brain waves can be divided in frequency bands
    • Related to different levels of activity
  • Resulting potentials on the scalp are in the order of 10µV – 100 µV
    • Prone to noise and artefacts
  • Custom instrumentation to reduce noise and ensure patient safety
    • Right leg driver, staged amplification
  • Clinical use includes sleep, coma and epilepsy monitoring

Electromyography (EMG)

  • The muscles are made up of muscle fiber bundles, individual fibres, myofibrils, sarcomeres and (thick and thin) filaments
  • Muscle contraction is triggered by a motor neuron, and causes via neurotransmission an action potential into T- tubules, provocing the release of Calcium, leading to filament interaction
  • EMG measured at the surface corresponds to superposition of several motor unit potentials
  • EMG also uses custom instrumentation to reduce noise and ensure patient safety
    • Right leg driver, staged amplification
  • Clinical use includes measuring muscle activity, and muscular and neurological lesions

Electroencephalography (ECG)

  • The heart is responsible for pumping the blood through two vascular circuits, ensuring oxygenation of the blood and perfusion of all tissues
  • The heart contains 4 chambers, 4 valves, and is connected to 2 arteries and 2 veins
  • ECG measured at the surface corresponds to depolarisation and repolarisation of atrium and ventricle heart muscles, and delays caused because of transfer of signals in the conduction system of the heart
  • Interpretation of the ECG allows to detect some minor and mayor pathologies such as fibrillation
  • Instrumentation also uses custom instrumentation to reduce noise and ensure patient safety
    • Right leg driver, staged amplification
