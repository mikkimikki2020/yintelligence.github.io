From sequence to structure - Secondary structure prediction

Princeton: Secondary structure prediction

Predictions 53:30

  • Model based
    • Preferably using some actual experimental data
  • Machine learning

Given a protein sequence with amino acids a1, a2, ..., an, the secondary structure prediction problem is to predict whether each amino acid ai is in an α−helix, a β−sheet, or neither.

If you know the actual secondary structure for each amino acid, then the 3-state accuracy is the percent of residues for which your prediction matches reality. It is called “3-state” because each residue can be in one of 3 “states”: α, β, or other （O）. Because there are only 3 states, random guessing would yield a 3-state accuracy of about 33% assuming that all structures are equally likely.

Chou-Fasman method 54:42

If you were asked to determine whether an amino acid in a protein of interest is part of a α-helix or β-sheet, you might think to look in a protein database and see which secondary structures amino acids in similar contexts belonged to.

The Chou-Fasman method （1978） is a combination of such statistics-based methods and rule-based methods.

  • Original attempt at predicting secondary structure
  • Chou and Fasman （1970s）
  • Based on very limited number of protein structures

Here are the steps of the Chou-Fasman algorithm:

Step 1. Calculate propensities from a set of solved structures

Statistical propensity for each amino acid （R） to adopt a certain secondary structure （S）: \[ Pr^R_S=\frac{Pr_{R,S}}{Pr_R}\ \ Pr_{R,S}=\frac{f_{R,S}}{f_S}\ \ Pr_R=\frac{f_R}{N} \]

N: total number amino acids

That is, we determine the probability that amino acid （R） is in each structure, normalized by the background probability that （R） occurs at all.

For example, let’s say that there are 20,000 amino acids in the database, of which 2000 are serine, and there are 5000 amino acids in helical conformation, of which 500 are serine. Then the helical propensity for serine is: \[ \frac{\frac{500}{5000}}{\frac{2000}{20000}}=1.0 \]

Step 2: Assign α/β propensities to each amino acid

Once the propensities are calculated, each amino acid is categorized using the propensities as one of:

  • helix-former
  • helix-breaker
  • helix-indifferent

That is, helix-formers have high helical propensities, helix-breakers have low helical propensities, and helix-indifferents have intermediate propensities.

Each amino acid is also categorized as one of:

  • sheet-former
  • sheet-breaker
  • sheet-indifferent

For example, it was found that glycine and prolines are helix-breakers.

When a sequence is input, find nucleation sites. These are short subsequences with a high-concentration of helix-formers （or sheet-formers）. These sites are found with some heuristic rule （e.g. “a sequence of 6 amino acids with at least 4 helix-formers, and no helix-breakers”）.

  1. Find region with 4/6 connected residues pα > 1.0
    • Extend on both ends until:
      • 4 connected residues have average pα < 1.0 → End of α helix
      • Total segment longer than 5 residues, average pα > pβ ?
    • Assign as α helix

Find region with 3/5 connected residues pβ > 1.0 - Extend on both ends until: - 4 connected residues have average pβ < 1.0 → End of β sheet - Average pβ > 1.05 and average pβ > pα? - Assign as β sheet

Repeat for whole sequence

  • If α/β overlap, choose which one by highest average p

Overall

Chou-Fasman algorithm:

  • Based on statistics
  • Very simple
    • Per-residue, no residue interactions included
    • Algorithm cutoffs are pretty arbitrary
  • Gives better than random results!
    • Q3 score of 42-52% depending on protein family

GOR method 1:11:22

To determine the structure for a given amino acid position j, the GOR method （named for the authors Garnier, Osguthorpe, Robson） looks at a window of n amino acids before and n after the position of interest.

为了确定给定氨基酸位置 j 的结构，GOR 方法（以作者 Garnier、Osguthorpe、Robson 命名）查看感兴趣位置之前和之后 n 个氨基酸的窗口。

  • Evolved from GOR to GOR-V

    • Jean Garnier, David Osguthorpe, Barry Robson

  • Based on information theory approach （Bayes）

    • Self-information
    • Directional information
    • Pair information

  • Clearly defined model to get to results

Self-information （related to Chou-Fasman）

\[ \begin{align} I(S;R) &=\log[\frac{P(S|R)}{P(S)}] \tag{1} \\ P(S|R) &= \frac{P(S,R)}{P(R)} \tag{2}\\ \end{align} \]

\[ P(S,R)=\frac{f_{S,R}}{N}\ P(R)=\frac{f_R}{N}\ P(S)=\frac{f_{S}}{N} \]

\[ \begin{align} I(S;R) &=\log(\frac{\frac{f_{S,R}}{f_R}}{\frac{f_{S}}{N}})\tag{3} \end{align} \]

Self-information, more general: \[ \begin{align} I(\Delta S;R) &=I(S;R)-I(n-S;R) \tag{1} \\ I(\Delta S;R) &= \log(\frac{f_{S,R}}{f_R}) + \log(\frac{f_{n-S}}{f_S})\tag{2}\\ \end{align} \]

Calculated for the three conformations, the highest value of Eq. （2） for one of the conformations S will be the predicted conformation and will be the propensity for that residue to be in that conformation, usually expressed in centonate units when natural logarithms are used.

对三种构象进行计算，其中一种构象 S 的方程式 2 的最高值将是预测的构象，并将是该残基处于该构象的倾向，通常在使用自然对数时以厘数单位表示。

Include local sequence information 包括本地序列信息

  • How often do you see a residue in relation to a secondary structure element at position j?

\[ I(ΔS_j;R_1,..., R_n ) = \log[\frac{P(S_j , R_1,..., R_n )}{P(n − S_j , R_1,..., R_n )}] +log[\frac{P(n − S)}{P(S)}] \]

GOR V 1:45:06

Uses multiple sequence alignment - Structure is more conserved than sequence - Insertions/deletions not as common in αhelices andβsheets, more so in loops. - PSI-BLAST on query sequence - Get set of sequences with high identity - Cutoff 30% if lots of hits, lower to 20% if not that many - Predict secondary structure for each sequence - Average predictions for each residue

  • Various other improvements
    • 513 proteins, 84107 residues （2002）

  • Has mean Q3 of 73.5%
  • Q3 ranges from 60.3% （allβ） to 84.3% (allα）
