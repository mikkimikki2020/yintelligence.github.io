DSSP (hydrogen bond estimation algorithm) 1:17:54

The DSSP algorithm is the standard method for assigning secondary structure to the amino acids of a protein, given the atomic-resolution coordinates of the protein.

DSSP begins by identifying the intra-backbone hydrogen bonds of the protein using a purely electrostatic definition, assuming partial charges of -0.42 e and +0.20 e to the carbonyl oxygen and amide hydrogen respectively, their opposites assigned to the carbonyl carbon and amide nitrogen. A hydrogen bond is identified if E in the following equation is less than -0.5 kcal/mol:

  • Simple definition for backbone hydrogen bond

    \[ E=0.084({\frac{1}{r_{ON}}+\frac{1}{r_{CH}}-\frac{1}{r_{OH}}-\frac{1}{r_{CN}}})·332kcal/mol \]

    • r is distance
    • N, H backbone atoms from residue i
    • O, C backbone atoms from residue j

Based on this, eight types of secondary structure are assigned.

  • α helix (H), i → i+4
  • β strand (E), long set of connected hydrogen bonds
  • 310 helix (G), i → i+3
  • And others…

Look at the local conformation of the amino acid.

Reference structure data set is crucial, because there are not too much overlap between sequences! In general, proteins with less than 25-30% sequence identity between them. Other considerations is about structure quality and origin, …

Secondary structure is a human concept! Atom coordinate information from tertiary structure is converted into discrete secondary structure states. This is what is used to develop secondary structure predictors.

Data preparation and cleanup

  1. What is the research question or problem?

    • Often determines the data you want to use

    • Prediction

      As clean as possible, better less but dependable

    • Statistical analysis

      Lots more of a bit lesser quality might be better

  2. There are many databases with similar data available, which one to use?

    • How up-to-date?
    • Where does the information come from?
    • How well curated is the data?
    • Is it already linked to other data you need?
    • Or create your own database

  3. Finding duplicate data and records

    • Are often present and you might not want duplication to avoid bias in your dataset

      E.g. lots of structures of same bacterial proteins in the PDB with different ligands

    • Or you might want to use duplicates

      E.g.same sequences but look at structure variation based on ligand binding or crystal lattice

  4. Standardization might be necessary

  5. Modifying legacy data

    Might be an older format that needs updating

  6. Standardizing nomenclature

    E.g. atom names might not be consistent in database, or need to be changed to what you use

  7. Clustering similar data

    • To avoid bias in for example machine learning

      For cross-validation

  8. Merging data records

  9. Filter out low quality or bad data

  10. Add some value if it is useful for analysis

    E.g.run different secondary structure determination programs （DSSP, STRIDE, …） on a set of structures from the PDB

  11. Consensus results are more work to generate but tend to be more relevant and robust

Q3 measure 35:35

\[ Q_3 = \frac{N_{residues\_correctly\_predicted}}{N_{residues\_total}} \]

  • Random prediction should give 0.33
  • Real random prediction gives 0.38
    • Frequencies of secondary structure are not the same
  • Bad predictions can give high scores ...

Matthews correlation coefficient (MCC) 40:48

Predicted for each secondary structure state, uses false/true positives and false/true negative

\[ MCC = \frac{TP×TN-TP×FN}{\sqrt{(TP+FP)(TP+FN)(TN+FP)(TN+FN)}} \]

  • Sensitivity (hit rate, recall) \[ TPR=\frac{TP}{FN} \]

  • Specificity \[ SPC = \frac{TN}{(FP + TN)} \]

Report MCC always per secondary structure class

  • Multi-class definition also available

\[ MCC_{multiclass} = \frac{\sum_k\sum_l\sum_mC_{kk}C_{lm}-C_{kl}C_{mk}}{\sqrt{\sum_k(\sum_lC_{kl})(\sum_{k'|k'≠k}\sum_{l'}C_{k'l'})}\sqrt{\sum_k(\sum_lC_{lk})(\sum_{k'|k'≠k}\sum_{l'}C_{l'k'})}} \]
