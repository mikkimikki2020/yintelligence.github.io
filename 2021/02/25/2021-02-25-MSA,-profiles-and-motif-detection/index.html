Multiple sequence alignment 25:42

An alignment of 2 sequences is an hypothesis that is supported or rejected by the score one obtains for the alignment, but…

  • the score does not guarantee that there really is a common ancestor
  • no guarantee that the elements are correctly aligned, only that the alignment is the one with maximum similarity

We can reduce this uncertainty by aligning additional sequences, a multiple sequence alignment （MSA） algorithm performs this task and can more clearly show:

  • The similarity between positions
  • The conservation of certain amino acids within a family of homologous proteins

The alignment of multiple sequences is a method of choice to detect conserved regions in protein or DNA sequences. These particular regions are usually associated with:

  • Signals （promoters, signatures for phosphorylation, cellular location, ...）;
  • Structure （correct folding, protein-protein interactions...）;
  • Chemical reactivity （catalytic sites,... ）.

Different methods exist to build models of these conserved regions:

  • Consensus sequences;
  • Patterns;
  • Position Specific Score Matrices （PSSMs）;
  • Profiles;
  • Hidden Markov Models （HMMs）,
  • ... and a few others

Why MSA? 30:14

  • Help prediction of the secondary and tertiary structures of proteins of new sequences

  • Help to find motifs or signatures characteristic of protein family

Multiple sequence alignment （MSA）:

  • Improve the alignment between two sequences

    The conserved regions: In green the identical residues and in blue the elements with the same properties

  • Conserved regions provide information on the similarities in structure and function which can be used to align sequences, search similar sequences in the databases or annotate new sequences.

Yet since the methods can only approximate the optimal solution, there can be differences between methods

Two important features of multiple alignment

  • Position specific scoring
  • The evolutionary relationship between sequences

Example

As shown in the picture, there are 8 fragments from immunoglobulin sequences.

Alignment highlights:

  • conserved residues
  • conserved regions
  • more sophisticated patterns, like the dominance of hydrophobic residues （V,L,I） at fragment positions 1 and 3.

The alignment can also enable us to infer the evolutionary history of the sequences. It looks like the first 4 sequences and the last 4 sequences are derived from 2 different common ancestors, that in turn derived from a "root" ancestor.

But true phylogentic analysis is more complex

The MSA problem 35:52

Determining the alignment between N sequences is a hard problem = Combinatorial optimisation problem (COP)

To solve this COP we need provide two systems:

  1. Calculating the score of the alignment
    • SP scores
  2. Algorithm that finds the MSA with the optimal （or close to optimal） score
    • An optimised version of the standard pairwise DP algorithms; limited applicability but exact
    • Progressive alignment （the CLUSTAL system）
    • Iterative improvement methods for multiple sequence alignment （the SAGA system）

The scoring system 36:28

How can we calculate the score of an MSA?

For pairwise alignments, we scored each column by looking at matches, mismatches, and gaps in the two sequences.

in practice, protein sequences are scored using substitution matrices.

Here, each column in an alignment is scored by summing the scores of all pairs of symbols in that column.

The score of column i （Sum-of-pairs or SP）: \[ S(m_{i})=\sum_{k,l}s(m^k_i,m^l_i) \]

  • mki is a residue in the sequence k in column i
  • s（mki, mli） The score from the substitution matrix

where scores s（a,b） come from a substitution scoring matrix such as a PAM or BLOSUM matrix. For simple linear gap costs, gaps are handled by defining s（a,-）and s（-,a）to be the gap cost. Otherwise gaps are scored separately.

The score of the entire alignment is then summed over all column scores: \[ S(m)=\sum_{i}S(m_{i}) \]

  • So the assumption is made that the score of one column i is independent of the other columns

Sum of pairs: SP scores

SP (Sum of Pair) score is the most widely used because its simplicity.

The following is a MSA of 4 sequences MQPILLLV, MLRLL, MKILLL, and MPPVLILV.

\[ \begin{align} SP(I,-,I,V) &= score(I,-) + score(I, I) +score(I,V) + score(-,I) + score (-,V) + score(I,V)\\ &= -2 + 1 + -1 + -2 + -2 + -1 \\ &= -7 \end{align} \] Although there is never an entire column of gaps, if we look at any 2 sequences in the alignment, there may be columns where both have gaps. The value of score（-,-） is set equal to 0; this eliminates double counting （or extra penalization） for the use of gaps.

Details: Princeton note

Minimum entropy: \[ S(m_i)=\sum_if^k_i\ln(f^k_i) \]

Progressive alignment: CLUSTAL system 40:20

CLUSTALW creates a multiple alignment by:

  1. a pairwise alignment of the two most similar sequences is performed, producing a cluster of two aligned sequences.

  2. This cluster can then be aligned to the next most related sequence or cluster of aligned sequences. Two clusters of sequences can be aligned by a simple extension of the pairwise alignment of two individual sequences.

  3. The final alignment is achieved by a series of progressive, pairwise alignments that include increasingly dissimilar sequences and clusters, until all sequences have been included in the final pairwise alignment.

    Before alignment, the sequences are first clustered by similarity to produce a dendrogram （tree representation） of clustering relationships. It is this dendrogram that directs the order of the subsequent pairwise alignments.

Progressive alignment problems 45:25

This method suffers from at least 2 problems:

  • The local minimum problem
    • New sequences are added on top of existing alignments.
    • Hence any early errors （misaligned regions） propagate to the complete alignment （due to divergence between sequences）
  • The choice of alignment parameters
    • One scoring matrix, a gap-open and a gap-extension value
    • This works often for highly related sequences, yet starts to fail seriously as soon as sequences diverge

Iterative improvement: SAGA system 46:22

Profiles 57:04

Using a profile we can better detect similarities between distantly related proteins.

Profiles store general information properties of a MSA :

  1. Frequency information concerning the residues in each column of the MSA
  2. The evolutionary importance of every residue in that position

Position specific scoring matrix (PSSM)

How to construct a PSSM ? 57:56

A PSSM is based on the frequencies of each residue in a specific position of a multiple alignment.

Determine the frequencies for each amino acid b in every column u for Nseq aligned sequences, take for instance:

\[ f_{2,R}=\frac{2}{3}=0.67\ \ f_{(i)2,R}=0.5\\ f_{6,E}=\frac{3}{3}=1\ \ f_{8,L}= \frac{1}{3} \]

\[ f_{u,b}=\frac{n_{u,b}}{N_{seq}}\\ f(i)_{u,b}=\frac{\ln(1-(\frac{n_{u,b}}{N_{seq}+1}))}{\ln(\frac{1}{N_{seq}+1})} \]

Different frequency-dependent calculations possible!

A profile stores for each column in the MSA, the frequency multiplied by the alignment score with every other AA. \[ m_{u,a}=\sum_{b∈\{AA\}}f_{u,b}S_{a,b} \]

mu,a is actually an alignment score between a residue a and the column u. sa,b is the matrix score.

Without the penalty line, the matrix is called a PSSM （Position-specific scoring matrix）

Probability of a match of a particular amino acid a with column u

can be calculated in different ways!

\[ m_{u,a}-\log\frac{q_{u,a}}{p_a} \ (same\ as \ pairwise) \]

This method is only useful when there are sufficient sequences and all amino acids appear at least once

The probability pa is again the overall probability that one can find a particular amino acid in any of the positions.

The score mu,a is the score for aligning a residue a with the column u

We can therefore use the same DP algorithm as before to align a new sequence to an existing MSA （profile）

The biggest problem in creating profiles is that the number of sequences can be too small, resulting in qu,a of zero, and as a consequence not every amino acids is present in each column \[ log 0 =-∞ \] As a result the alignment can not be made using the log-formula （see PAM and BLOSUM discussion）

  • →PSEUDOCOUNTS

Pseudo-counts

Pseudo-counts are constants that one adds to the values in the profile to avoid zero’s. \[ q_{u,a}=\frac{n_{u,a}+1}{N_{seq}+20} \]

Pseudo-counts provide the prior information that we have on the amino acids

Thus, qu,a will never be 0 ! \[ β=\sqrt{N_{seq}}\\ q_{u,a}=\frac{n_{u,a}+βp_a}{N_{seq}+β} \]

β is weighting factor that tunes the importance of the pseudo-counts in the value of qu,a

Calculating pa

We can further generalize this function and introduce fu,a directly \[ q_{u,a}=\frac{\alpha f_{u,a}+βp_a}{\alpha +β} \]

α is also a weighting factor that tunes the importance of the actual data.

Sometimes α = Nseq-1 is used

When there is no data （no sequence） then the pseudo-counts will determine the values in the profiles

Therefore, pseudo-counts correspond to the prior distribution, which corresponds to what we know about the system before introducing the data.

Once the profile is determined one can also derive a consensus sequence which contains for every column the amino acid with the highest score mu,a

Also possible to calculate the information content for every column u in the sequence （log base 2, bitscore） \[ I_u=\log_220-H_u \]

information content

\[ H_u=-\sum f_{u,a}\log_2f_{u,a} \]

uncertainty

A position with one highly conserved amino acid has the lowest uncertainty, and the maximum information content.

The contribution of each amino acid to this score is given by fu,aIu.

Aligning to a profile 1:33:17

Simply use either Needleman-Wunsch （L3） or Smith-Waterman （L3） to make the alignment

The profile contains now the alignment scores

The major difficulty lies in the way in which gap penalties are assigned.

But now use the information in the PSSM to calculate the score (linear gap penalty): \[ S(i,j)= \begin{cases} S(i-1,j-1)+PSSM(seq(i)),j)\\ S(i-1,j)+PSSM("-",j)\\ S(i,j-1)+PSSM("-",j-1)\\ 0 \end{cases} \]

  • j is column in PSSM
  • PSSM（“-“,j） is penalty for gap in position j
  • seq（i） is the amino acid in position i of the sequence

Use in your project the following formula for calculating the values in your PSSM

\[ m_[u,a]=\log\frac{q_{u,a}}{p_a} \] With: \[ q_{u,a}=\frac{\alpha f_{u,a}+βp_a}{\alpha +β}\\ \alpha=N_{seq}-1\\ β=\sqrt{N_{seq}} \]

PSSM: Conclusion

  • Advantages:
    • Good for short, conserved regions.
    • Relatively fast and simple to implement.
    • Produce match scores that can be interpreted based on statistical theory.
  • Limitations:
    • Insertions and deletions are strictly forbidden.
    • Relatively long sequence regions can therefore not be described with this method.
  • When I use it?
    • To model small regions with high variability but constant length.
