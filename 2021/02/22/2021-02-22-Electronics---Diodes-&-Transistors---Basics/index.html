The Diode 9:38

The diode is the simplest semiconductor component, containing two leads. The diode is visualised with an arrow. This arrow visualises in which way the current can flow through the diode, since the diode only accepts one way of current flow.

Goal

  • understand how a diode is built
  • draw the I-V characteristic curve of a diode
  • know the equivalent circuit of a diode as nonlinear element
  • use the diode as a switch in a rectifier circuit

How is a diode built?

Physically the diode is realised by the combination of two types of semiconductors

  • P-type semiconductor is one where the free carriers are positively charged.
  • N-type semiconductors have negatively charged free carriers. Where the two types of semiconductor meet, is called the diode junction.

How does a diode work? 10:51 - 18:37

How does the applied voltage affect the current?

在上图中，正电压 V 施加在二极管上。 由于该电压，存在电场 E 使正负载流子彼此加速。在junction处，这两种类型的free carriers相遇并cancel out through recombination。 在左侧二极管中流动的正电流是由从左向右移动的正载流子产生的。流出的正电流是因为从右向左移动的负载流子。 在这种情况下，二极管表现为正向偏置 forward bias,，以正确的箭头方向通过电流。

在上图中，在二极管上施加负电压 V。 这会产生一个电场，使负载流子和正载流子远离junction。 该junction现在充当insulator，几乎没有剩余的自由载流子。 该绝缘体具有高resistance，通过二极管的电流将非常低。 在这种情况下，二极管在反向偏置reverse bias中起作用。

The built-in potential in a semiconductor equals the potential across the depletion region in thermal equilibrium（半导体中的内建电势等于热平衡中耗尽区的电势）。

Since thermal equilibrium implies that the Fermi energy is constant throughout the p-n diode, the built-in potential equals the difference between the Fermi energies, EFn and EFp, divided by the electronic charge.

It also equals the sum of the bulk potentials of each region, ϕn and ϕp, since the bulk potential quantifies the distance between the Fermi energy and the intrinsic energy.

Characteristics of a diode 18:40

Current-Voltage characteristic (I-V curve)

For positive voltages, the current is also positive and increases exponentially with the applied voltage.

The current starts flowing when the voltage is above the threshold voltage Vd. This threshold voltage is dependent on the used semiconductor, but since most diodes are made with a silicon semiconductor, the typical threshold voltage of a diode is the one for Si, Vd=0.7 V.

For an applied voltage equal to zero, there will be no current flow in the diode. For negative voltages, there will be a small negative leakage current –IS, this is called the saturation current of the diode.

With q equals the electron charge, k equals the constant of Boltzmann, T the absolute temperature and η an empirical constant called the ideality factor that is dependent on the used semiconductor in the diode.

For semiconductors with a large bandgap (such as silicon) η=2. For smaller bandgap semiconductors (like germanium) η=1

From the diode characteristic we can define the application possibilities for a diode.

  • Use the diode as a switch, it behaves as an element that conducts current completely (low resistance), or doesn’t conduct current at all (very high resistance).
  • In more complicated applications it can be used as a general nonlinear element.

The diode as a nonlinear element 23:00

The use of a diode as a general nonlinear element is visualised.

In this case the applied voltage over the diode is non-ideal, with a direct voltage U, an alternating voltage vw(t) and a non-zero load resistance R. The voltage over the diode can be split up in an average time independent value and a time dependent signal component, VD(t)=VD + vD(t). The same happens with the current through the diode, ID(t)=ID + iD(t).

The analysis of the circuit in happens in two steps.

  • In the first step, called the DC analysis, the time independent average values of voltages and currents are calculated.
  • In the second step a linearization is done for small variations around the DC point or bias point, this is called the small signal model.

DC Analysis

For the DC analysis all the alternating components are set to zero.

This gives the simplified circuit:

The time independent values over the diode VD and ID have to fulfil two characteristics which are visualised.

  • The diode characteristic is blue
  • In the external circuit, U and R are linear elements and thus they have a linear curve between VD and ID.
  • This straight line in the VD, ID plane is called the resistive load line.

To draw this line, at least 2 points need to be known.

  • For the first point an open circuit is made, this means that ID = 0, There is no current flow through R and VD = U. The intersection of the resistive load line and the VD axis (this means ID=0) is VD=U.
  • For the second point VD is put to zero (short circuit) in the external circuit. The current through R will be ID=U/R. This is the intersection of the resistive load line and the current axis. If the two points are connected, we get the complete resistive load line (red).

The intersection between both characteristics (diode curve and resistive load line) gives the DC values of VD and ID of the voltage VD(t) and the current ID(t) in the circuit. The coordinates (VD, ID) is called the bias point, operating point or Q-point of the circuit.

AC equivalent model / small signal model

For AC voltages that differ from zero in the circuit, VD(t) and ID(t) will fluctuate around the bias point. The point (VD(t), ID(t)) is an element of the diode curve (blue). When the fluctuations are not too large around the bias point, the nonlinear diode curve in blue can be replaced by the tangent of this diode curve in the bias point. In this way we have a linear approximation of our nonlinear diode curve that works for small fluctuations around the bias point.

Conclusion: the signal components vD(t) and iD(t) of the diode behave as a resistance RD.

For the calculation of the components in the circuit, the small signal model can be used as visualised.

The diode is replaced by the dynamic resistance and all the DC components are removed from the circuit. The removal of the DC voltage source U (U=0) is equal to a short circuit. A capacitance is added for signals with high frequencies.

Summary

A small summary of how a time dependent nonlinear circuit can be handled:

  • DC analysis: analysis of the time independent nonlinear circuit.
  • AC analysis/small signal model: analysis of the time dependent linear circuit.
  • The original voltage and current is the sum of the DC component from the first step and the signal component from the second step.

The Bipolar Junction Transistor (BJT) 39:40

Goals

  • Know how a bipolar transistor is built.
  • Know the input and output characteristics of the transistor.
  • Know the equivalent circuit of a transistor.
  • Know the basic transistor circuits for voltage and current amplification.

Introduction 40:16

A type of transistor that uses both electrons and holes as charge carriers. A BJT allows a small current injected at one of its terminals to control a much larger current flowing between two other terminals, making the device capable of amplification or switching.

The first bipolar transistor was invented at Bell Labs by William Shockley, Walter Brattain, and John Bardeen in 1947. They were awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1956 for the transistor.

Bipolar transistors are mostly used for amplification, power regulation, at small scale.

Basic structure & symbols 42:22

Like the diode is built from two semiconductor layers, so is the bipolar transistor build from three semiconductor layers. Two types of bipolar transistors exist, dependent on the used n-type or p-type semiconductors. This is visualised in Figure.

The transistor has three external leads: the Collector, Emitter and Base.

  • The main current flows through the Collector and Emitter lead.
  • The direction of the arrow in the symbols of Figure indicate the normal current flow.
  • The amount of current through Collector and Emitter can be manipulated through a small current through the Base.

The characteristics of the transistor can be described when two independent voltages and two independent currents are known.

  • For the independent currents this will be the Base current Ib and the Collector current Ic. The independent voltages are the Base-Emitter voltage Vbe and the Emitter-Collector voltage Vce.
  • The current that flows out of the Emitter will be Ib + Ic according to the current law of Kirchhoff, and the Base-Collector voltage will be Vce-Vbe according to the voltage law of Kirchhoff

Detailed structure 50:05

NPN transistor structures (not to scale)

An NPN transistor is realised by a thin p-type semiconductor layer in between two n-type semiconductor layers.

  • The collector is lightly doped so that the collector-base junction will have a high breakdown voltage.
  • The collector also needs to be heavily doped to minimize ohmic losses for high currents. → contradiction → heavy doping at contact only
  • The collector current travels shorter through the player than the Base current → 𝐼𝐵 ≪ 𝐼C
  • The current voltage characteristic Ib=f(Vbe) will be the same as a diode I-V curve. The Base region is the thin p-layer in between the two n-layers of the Emitter and Collector. Due to this reason the Collector current travels shorter through the p-layer than the Base current. Here out it can be proven that the ratio Ib/Ic is much smaller than one and almost constant.

PNP transistor structures

The PNP transistor is realised by joining two p-layers around one thin n-layer. The Base region is connected to the n-layer. The Collector and Emitter are each connected to one of two p-layers. The operation of a PNP transistor is likewise to an NPN transistor. Between Emitter and Base a diode characteristic exist –Ib=f(-Vbe). –Ib and –Ic are positive. The ratio (–Ib)/(– Ic) is smaller than one and almost constant.

Current Flow

Main components 47:58

Main current = collector current: electrons flowing from emitter to collector. Modulated by bias voltage between base and emitter.

We want to minimize base-emitter current (maximize amplification).

  • Recombination of injected electrons with holes in B region
  • Recombination at the base-emitter depletion region
  • Hole injection from base into emitter

Characteristics of a BJT 52:28

Characteristic curves

Current-controlled

The operation of a transistor is described by the input or Base-Emitter characteristic and the output or Collector-Emitter characteristic.

  • The input characteristic has the shape of a diode characteristic.

  • The output characteristic shows the Collector current Ic in function of the Collector-Emitter voltage Vce (which is dependent on the value of the Base current Ib).

    • If Vce > 0,2 V (NPN transistor) or –Vce > 0,2 V(PNP transistor) then the ratio Ic/Ib is constant. This ratio is called the common-Emitter current gain of the transistor and is indicated by the symbol β.

      A typical value for the common-Emitter current gain β= Ic/Ib≈100.

Equivalent diagrams 56:15

When we take a look at the operation of an NPN transistor, it is clear that if Vce > 0.2 V, the transistor can be replaced by the equivalent circuit. This equivalent circuit has the same external behaviour as the transistor. Between Base and Emitter, the transistor is replaced by a diode. Between Collector and Emitter the transistor is replaced by a current source with value βIb. The transistor behaves as a current-controlled current source.

The same can be done for a PNP transistor, when -Vce > 0.2V an equivalent circuit exist as visualised. Between Base and Emitter a diode is placed, between Collector and Emitter a current source is added with value β(-Ib).

Current gain parameters 58:02

By combining the two parameters α and β we can produce two mathematical expressions that describe the relationship between the different currents flowing in the transistor.

The values of Beta vary from about 20 for high current power transistors to well over 1000 for high frequency low power type bipolar transistors. The value of Beta for most standard NPN transistors can be found in the manufactures data sheets but generally range between 50 – 200.

DC bias point and AC small signal model 1:05:54

The bipolar transistor is a nonlinear element due to the presence of the diodes in the characteristics.

The analysis for the nonlinear transistor can be handled in the same way as the analysis for the diode.

First, we look for the DC bias point, afterwards we perform an AC small signal model.

In a first step the DC components of the currents and voltages are being considered, as visualised in the middle. For this DC component the general nonlinear equivalent circuit is studied. From this DC components the DC bias point of the transistor is determined.

In the second step the time dependent AC components are being considered around the time independent bias point. If the AC variations vbe(t) and ib(t) are small enough, the nonlinear characteristics at the input, the diode, can be made linear around the bias point, this is called the linearization. This means that if vbe(t) and ib(t) are small, the diode can be replaced by an equivalent dynamic resistance as seen in equations.

This dynamic resistance of the diode is given by equation: Rd=25mV/Ib. The complete time dependent currents and voltages in the transistor are the sum of the DC bias point , from a nonlinear time independent analysis, and the AC small signal model, from a time dependent linear analysis.

Introduction

The FET is a type of transistor that uses an electric field to control the flow of current. FETs are unipolar devices with three terminals. FETs control the flow of current by the application of a voltage to the gate terminal, thereby altering the conductivity between the drain and source.

The concept of a field-effect transistor (FET) was first patented by Julius Edgar Lilienfeld in 1925 and by Oskar Heil in 1934, but they were unable to build a working practical semiconducting device. The first FET device to be successfully built was the junction field-effect transistor in 1945.

The most important applications are integrated circuits (for switching). Thanks to their high input impendence, they are often preferred in complex systems.

Basic structure & symbols 1:18:19

MOSFET

Two different kind of field effect transistors exist:

  • The metal-oxide-silicon field effect transistor=MOSFET.

    The name indicates the structure of the field effect transistor.

  • The junction field effect transistor=JFET

  • N-channel enhancement MOSFET
  • N-channel depletion MOSFET
  • P-channel enhancement MOSFET
  • P-channel depletion MOSFET

A MOSFET is a component with three external leads: the Gate(G), Drain (D) and the Source (S). In between the Drain and the Source a channel may exist that can conduct current. When this channel consists of negative free carriers, we call it an n-channel MOSFET. When it consists of positive carriers, it is called a p-channel MOSFET. The direction of the arrow in the MOSFET symbol indicates the type of channel: for an n-channel, the arrow points from Drain to Source, for a p-channel the arrow points from Source to Drain.

The possible existence of a channel is controlled by the voltage applied over the Gate. When a channel exists without a Gate voltage, this is called a depletion MOSFET. When a channel can’t exist without a Gate voltage, this is called an enhancement MOSFET. In the symbol of an enhancement MOSFET, the channel is indicated with a dotted line. The depletion MOSFET has a full line to visualise the channel.

Because the MOSFET has three external leads, we can choose two independent currents and two independent voltages to characterize the MOSFET. For the independent currents we choose the Gate current Ig and the Drain current Id.

For alternating currents with low frequencies, the Gate current will be equal to zero, Ig=0. Taken the law of Kirchhoff into account, this means that the current flow out of the Source will be equal to the Drain current Id. The independent voltages are the Gate-Source Vgs and the Drain-Source Vds. Following the law of Kirchhoff, this means the voltage between the Gate and Drain will be Vds-Vgs.

Characteristics of a FET

Input-output characteristics 1:23:30

The connection between Gate and Source is called the input of the field effect transistor. The input characteristic gives the relation between Ig and Vgs. Connected to the Gate is always an insulator: with the MOSFET this is an oxide, with the JFET this is a junction diode with a negative voltage. This means there is never a current flow through the input of the field effect transistor, Ig=0.

The connection between Drain and Source is considered to be the output of the field effect transistor. The output characteristics gives the relation between Id and Vds. This relationship is in function of the input voltage Vgs. The general output characteristic of a field effect transistor is visualised. One solid line represents the output characteristic for one specific input voltage Vgs. For an n-channel transistor following conditions apply:

  • Close to the origin is a linear relation between the output current Id and the output voltage Vds. This indicates that the output of the field effect transistor behaves as a resistor. The slope of this linear relation is the resistor value and is dependent on the input voltage Vgs. Now one can see that the output resistor is adjustable through the input voltage: Vds/Id=R(Vgs). The bigger the input voltage Vgs, the more free carriers are present in the n-channel, the lower the resistance R(Vgs) of the channel.

  • The output current Id is function of the output voltage Vds, as long as Vds0. For a depletion n-channel field effect transistor: Vt<0

  • When Vds>Vgs-Vt, the output current will be independent of the output voltage Vds (horizontal part of the characteristic). This indicates that the output of the transistor behaves as a current source.

    The amount of current the current source delivers is dependent on the input voltage Vgs, this is called a voltage induced current source, Id=f(Vgs). The higher Vgs, the more carriers in the channel, and a bigger current flow between Drain and Source.
